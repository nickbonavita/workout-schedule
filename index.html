General Workout Advice

Lifting Targets

  • Perform 3 to 4 sets per exercise.
  • Choose a weight that brings you close to failure between reps 8 and 12.
  • If form breaks down late in the set, count that as your effective failure point.

Execution Standard

  • Every rep should be slow, controlled, and intentional.
  • Avoid using speed or momentum to move the weight.

Weekly Structure

  • Use a three-day cycle: Push, Pull, Legs.
  • Core and cardio can be added to those days or trained on separate days.
  • Include at least some light cardio every day.

Heart Rate Zones

  • Use heart rate zones to guide training intensity from easy to near-max effort.
  • Keep roughly 80% of your cardio work in Zone 2 (easy to moderate, steady pace).
  • Use the remaining 20% in Zone 4/5 (hard to very hard intervals or efforts).

Core Training Approach

  • Core work fits well into circuits where you move continuously between exercises with minimal rest.
  • Use higher rep ranges for dynamic movements and time goals for holds like planks.
  • For planks and similar isometrics, set clear targets (for example, total hold time or timed rounds) and progress gradually.

Programming Tips

  • Keep form strict and breathing controlled to maintain quality as fatigue builds.
  • Increase challenge over time by adding reps, extending hold times, or reducing rest intervals.

General Lifestyle Advice

Calories

  • Calorie balance is the number one driver of weight control.
  • Consistently eating above maintenance tends to increase body weight, while eating below maintenance tends to reduce it.

Macronutrients

  • Protein supports muscle growth, repair, and recovery.
  • For weight lifting, a practical protein target is about 0.75 × body weight (in pounds) as grams per day.
  • Example: a 200-pound person should aim for about 150 grams of protein per day.
  • Carbohydrates fuel performance and are especially useful for cardio and higher-volume training.
  • Fats are essential for hormonal balance, nutrient absorption, and overall health.
  • All three macronutrients matter and should be included in a balanced plan.

What to Look Out For

  • Try to build meals around whole grains, with a strong baseline goal of at least 50% of your grain intake coming from whole-grain sources.
  • Fiber helps digestion, satiety, blood sugar control, and long-term heart health.
  • Aim for roughly 15 to 20 grams of fiber per day as a minimum target.
  • High intakes of added sugar can make calorie control harder and may negatively affect metabolic health over time.
  • Keep added sugar under about 35 grams per day.
  • Excess saturated fat can be problematic for cardiovascular health in some people.
  • Keep saturated fat under 20 grams per day, and lower is generally better when practical.
  • Avoid trans fat entirely whenever possible. The safe daily target is 0 grams.
  • Prioritize unsaturated fats and whole grains to support better energy, heart health, and dietary quality.

Daily Movement Habits

  • Build movement into your day by attaching it to routines you already follow.
  • The easiest way to improve lifestyle activity is to attach movement to routines you already do every day.
  • Take a short walk around the block with your morning coffee.
  • Go for a brief stroll after meals to support insulin sensitivity and post-meal blood sugar control.
  • Park farther away than usual to add low-effort steps throughout the day.
  • Small, repeatable habits often produce better long-term results than occasional all-or-nothing efforts.
